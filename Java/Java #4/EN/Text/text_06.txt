In modern software development, time management requires much more than simply saving a specific moment to a database. In most business scenarios, we need to calculate time intervals (differences), such as finding out how many days are left until a subscription expires, measuring how many milliseconds a process or algorithm takes, or adding a specific duration like "3 months and 15 days" to the current date. In the past, performing these calculations manually using mathematical operations (like dividing and multiplying milliseconds) often led to critical errors due to leap years, varying month lengths, and daylight saving time transitions. To completely eliminate this complexity and risk of error, Java provides the Period and Duration classes, which allow us to manage time intervals in a highly safe, clear, and standardized manner. The basic rule is simple: the Period class is used for broader, calendar- and date-based measurements (years, months, days), while the Duration class is preferred for narrower, time-based, and precise measurements (hours, minutes, seconds, nanoseconds).
At the core of how these two classes operate is the concept of measuring the "distance" between two different points on the timeline. As highlighted on the left side of the slide, the Period class structurally works with LocalDate objects. The Duration class, on the other hand, integrates with LocalTime or LocalDateTime objects that contain time information. To find this distance between two points in time, both classes commonly use the static between() method. Additionally, these classes do not just find the difference between two dates; they can also be used in conjunction with manipulation methods like plus() and minus() to add or subtract a specific amount of time from an existing date or time. Thanks to this modern architecture, all that complex background calendar math is managed automatically by Java.
The process of calculating a time interval and translating it into code follows a logical workflow, as detailed in the middle and right sections of the slide. The process first begins with the creation of the start and end times in memory to be compared; for example, in the code, a past date LocalDate.of(2022, 5, 10) and the current date LocalDate.now() are defined. Since the operation we are performing is finding a date difference, the appropriate class is selected, and by calling the Period.between(oldDate, currentDate) method, the net difference is calculated and assigned to a new 'period' object. Once the difference is stored in memory, the specific details within this object are accessed in parts using relevant methods such as getYears(), getMonths(), and getDays(). Similarly, to find time differences, LocalTime objects are defined, the elapsed time is calculated with Duration.between(), and the hour or minute equivalents of this duration are obtained using the toHours() or toMinutes() methods. In the final stage, these precise and reliable time difference data are printed to the console using the System.out.println() method, or they become ready to be used in a different business logic required by the application (for example, calculating the late fee of an invoice).
