Let's talk about math. As developers, simply storing data isn't enough; we have to manipulate it. On this slide, we are diving into Java's Arithmetic Operators. The good news is, if you remember your basic algebra, you're already halfway there.
Starting on the left side, we have our daily mathematical toolkit. Addition, subtraction, and multiplication use the standard symbols you'd expect. However, Java has two specific 'gotchas' you need to memorize. First, look at the division operator. When you divide two integers in Java, it doesn't give you fractions. It ruthlessly chops off the decimals. So, five divided by two gives you exactly two. Next is the percent sign. In Java, this isn't a percentage; it’s the Modulo operator. It calculates the leftover remainder of a division. For instance, it's a developer's favorite shortcut to figure out if a user's input is an odd or even number.
But what happens when you throw all these symbols into one long, messy equation? Who goes first? That brings us to the middle section: Operator Precedence. This flowchart acts as Java's referee. Parentheses are the absolute kings—whatever is inside them gets calculated first, period. Next in line are the increment and decrement operators. After that, multiplication, division, and modulo operations take priority, scanning from left to right. Finally, sitting at the bottom of the hierarchy, regular addition and subtraction are evaluated last.
To see why this is so critical, let’s look at the actual code on the far right. We start with variables a as 10 and b as 3. The basic operations execute as expected: 10 divided by 3 gives us a flat 3 because the decimals are dropped, and 10 % 3 leaves us with a remainder of 1.
But look closely at the 'Precedence Rules' section. This is where bugs often hide. In the line a + b * 2, Java follows the hierarchy, multiplying 3 by 2 first, resulting in 16. But in the very next line, just by adding parentheses to (a + b), we force the program to do the addition first, and the result completely changes to 26! Finally, we see a neat little trick at the bottom: the counter++ increment operator. It's a fast, clean way to simply add 1 to a value, turning our 5 into a 6 before printing everything to the screen."
